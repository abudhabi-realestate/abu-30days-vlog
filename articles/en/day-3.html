Day 3 | Who Is Buying Abu Dhabi Property? Locals vs International, 71% Off-Plan

Abu Dhabi Real Estate 30 Days · Day 3 · Based on ADREC 2025 public data

Opening

Day 1 was the map. Day 2 was "why look at Abu Dhabi at all."

Today, a different angle — not "where to buy," but "who is buying."

Many people watching Abu Dhabi get stuck on one question:

"Is this market only locals? Are foreigners really entering? Off-plan is 70% — is that a bubble?"

Day 3: ADREC 2025 official data, buyer structure broken down.

Understanding who is buying matters more than average price — because it directly answers: Are you that kind of buyer?

Body Structure

01 | Conclusion First: Three Buyer Types, One Shared Pattern

ADREC splits residential transactions by buyer identity into three groups: Emirati nationals, Resident Foreign, and Non-Resident Foreign (FDI / overseas investment).

In 2025, Abu Dhabi residential transaction value totaled AED 76.1 billion, up 67% y-o-y — a record. By value:

Resident Foreign: 51% (AED 38.9 billion) — the absolute core. Foreigners working in the UAE on visas and living here long-term are the main buyers of three-island apartments, Al Raha coastal communities, and Yas villas.

Emirati nationals: 38% (AED 28.9 billion) — mainland villa communities, some emerging island villas, and large government-planned communities; local family demand is real and sticky.

Non-Resident Foreign (FDI): 11% (AED 8.2 billion) — smaller in size, but concentrated in Saadiyat, Yas, and other ultra-luxury and tourism zones; typical profile is overseas pure investment and HNW allocation.

Budget, hold period, and exit logic differ completely across the three — but they share one move: buying off-plan.

ADREC 2025 full-year data: 71% of residential transactions were off-plan. Seven out of ten homes sold were not yet built.

Abu Dhabi's default option today isn't "pick up cheap ready stock" — it's betting on the city delivering in 3—5 years. That buyer temperament is very different from Dubai's current tilt toward "ready stock + short turnover."

02 | Buyer Structure Is Shifting: Foreign Buyers Are Driving Incremental Growth

2025 static ratios aren't enough — the trend is clearer.

When freehold opened to foreigners in 2019, Emirati buyers accounted for 63% of residential transactions, Resident Foreign 34%, FDI only 5%.

By 2025, Emirati share fell to 38%, Resident Foreign rose to 51%, FDI to 11%.

From 2022—2025, 69% of incremental residential transaction growth came from Resident Foreign and Non-Resident Foreign purchases — foreign buyers aren't "decorating" the market; they're driving growth.

Resident Foreign purchase value rose about 5.4x; FDI about 8.2x.

ADREC also notes: top 10 nationalities account for about 60% of FDI purchases; top 10 account for about 65% of Resident Foreign purchases — buyers aren't scattered; there are clear country clusters.

Abu Dhabi isn't a market where "locals keep to themselves." Foreign buyers have moved from the edge to the mainstream — but the mainstream is people already living in the UAE, not short-term flippers.

03 | Which Countries Are Buying? China Appears on Both Lists

The ADREC 2025 report publishes Top 10 nationalities for FDI and Resident Foreign separately (alphabetical order, not ranked by volume).

Non-Resident Foreign (FDI) Top 10 nationalities include:

Canada, China, France, Germany, India, Jordan, Kazakhstan, Lebanon, Russia, Switzerland, Syria, UK, US

Resident Foreign (Expat) Top 10 nationalities include:

Canada, China, Egypt, France, Germany, India, Jordan, Kazakhstan, Lebanon, Russia, Switzerland, Syria, UK, US

Points worth noting for Chinese buyers:

First, China appears on both lists. Chinese buyers in Abu Dhabi aren't "isolated cases" — there are people already resident in the UAE working and living here, and people allocating from overseas directly.

Second, India, UK, US, France, Russia, Canada, and others repeat on both lists — Abu Dhabi's buyer pool is genuinely multi-national, not a one-country show.

Third, FDI top 10 nationalities contribute about 60% of FDI transaction value; Resident Foreign top 10 about 65% — if you're entering from overseas, your "fellow travelers" are mainly allocation capital from these countries, not local retail buyers.

For Chinese buyers, the key isn't "are Chinese buying?" — it's whether you're Resident or FDI type — that determines which areas to look at and what hold logic to use.

04 | Local vs International: Different Zones, Different Buyers

"Who is buying Abu Dhabi?" has no single answer — it depends on the zone.

ADREC breaks down buyer composition in the Top 10 transaction zones. Key numbers:

Al Saadiyat Island: FDI accounts for 25% of zone transactions — among the highest FDI concentrations citywide. Saadiyat and Yas together account for 59% of citywide FDI transaction value. The main destination for overseas HNW allocation is here.

Yas Island: FDI 16% — tourism + lifestyle; both international buyers and resident families, but FDI share lower than Saadiyat.

Al Reem Island: Resident Foreign 75% — financial district + white-collar long-stay logic; most buyers are "people who work here."

Al Raha: Resident Foreign 84% — among the highest Resident Foreign shares citywide; classic "long-stay expat family community" — buying a life, not a story.

Top 10 transaction zones combined: about 53% of transaction value driven by Resident Foreign — again confirming Abu Dhabi's base is "people already here."

Emirati buyers show up more in Al Hudayriyyat, Al Bahyah, Zayed City, and other mainland and emerging villa communities — in 2025 Hudayriyat was already the #2 transaction zone citywide, with multiple flagship villa launches clustered.

Overseas FDI concentrates in Saadiyat / Yas; Resident Foreign fills Reem / Raha; Emirati buyers support mainland villa belts. Three buyer types, three territories — not fighting over the same inventory.

05 | 71% Off-Plan, 87% Cash — What Does That Mean?

Many people tense up at "71% off-plan" — is everyone gambling?

ADREC data gives a fuller picture:

Off-plan dominates because the market is young. Abu Dhabi only opened freehold at scale in 2019; new launches far exceed mature secondary stock. In 2025 residential transactions, 71% were off-plan — apartment off-plan sales rose 125% in 2025, 85% concentrated in Saadiyat, Yas, ADGM (Reem + Maryah), and Fahid.

Off-plan ≠ leveraged speculation. In the same period, 87% of residential transactions were all-cash — Saadiyat, Hudayriyat, Reem, Yas, Fahid, and other off-plan-heavy zones had especially high cash ratios. Buyers aren't betting on "10% down, 10x return" — they're using cash to secure core assets.

Off-plan buyers bet on 3—5 years. The top 10 developers in 2025 took 90%+ of off-plan primary sales (about AED 50 billion) — the market is led by government-linked master developers like Aldar; buyers are buying master-planned community delivery, not small-developer lottery tickets.

Ready stock is rising too. 2025 ready transactions up 44% y-o-y — end-user and "move in / rent now" demand is also growing. Ready apartments: 80% concentrated in ADGM, Al Raha, and Saadiyat.

But honestly: high off-plan share ≠ no risk. Delivery delays, quality gaps, non-core liquidity — Day 18 covers that. Day 3 emphasizes one point: if you only accept "buy today, rent tomorrow, sell anytime," Abu Dhabi's current 71% off-plan structure may not fit you.

06 | vs Dubai: Why the Buyer Pools Differ

Day 2 said "buyer pools are completely different" — ADREC data adds another layer:

Dubai is more global-brand driven: mature short-stay narrative, fast international hotspot rotation, relatively higher speculation density.

Abu Dhabi is more government-led supply + resident population absorption: 51% of 2025 transactions from Resident Foreign, Emirati buyers still 38% — the local base is thicker than many assume.

Population supports demand. Abu Dhabi population about 4.2 million in 2024, up 51% over ten years; employment growth over 9% in 2024. The UAE overall has 200+ nationalities in the resident population — buyer and tenant pools are built on real inflows, not paper stories.

Reem + Yas still about 40% of citywide transaction volume — highly concentrated in core zones; "there are buyers" and "your unit has buyers" are two different things.

Abu Dhabi isn't a market propped up by hotspot traffic — it's resident population + government planning + multi-national residents digesting supply together.

07 | Four Common Misconceptions Among Chinese Buyers

Misconception 1: "Abu Dhabi is the budget version of Dubai"

Buyer structure doesn't support that. 51% Resident Foreign, 11% FDI — people are buying capital-city credit + tight supply + long-hold logic, not "Dubai is too expensive, so second best."

Misconception 2: "70% off-plan = bubble"

71% off-plan + 87% cash — that's early-build phase + cash positioning, not a leverage bubble. Watch non-core off-plan, delivery risk, and treating "sold out" as "guaranteed profit."

Misconception 3: "Apply Dubai short-stay rental logic to Abu Dhabi"

51% Resident Foreign in the buyer pool; Raha 84% long-stay families — more "people who live here" than "people here for two nights." Abu Dhabi is long-rental dominant; don't simply copy the Dubai Airbnb model.

Misconception 4: "Foreigners barely buy in Abu Dhabi"

2025: 62% of residential transactions from foreign buyers (51% resident + 11% FDI), contributing 69% of 2022—2025 incremental growth. China appears on both FDI and Resident Foreign Top 10 lists — it's not that nobody is buying; you may not be in the right zone yet.

08 | Which Buyer Are You?

Question 1: Do you need long-term UAE residency?

Yes → Resident Foreign logic; prioritize Reem, Raha, Yas lifestyle circles — 75%—84% of buyers in these zones are your type.

No → FDI logic; prioritize Saadiyat (FDI 25%) and Yas (FDI 16%); accept lower turnover.

Question 2: Can you accept 3—5 year off-plan?

Yes → you match the current 71% mainstream — off-plan is the main way to secure core locations.

No → focus on ready secondary; 2025 ready sales up 44%, but 80% in ADGM, Raha, Saadiyat — narrower choice than off-plan.

Question 3: Liquidity or cycle resilience?

Liquidity → Reem apartments, among the highest transaction volumes citywide; Reem + Yas ~40% volume combined.

Cycle resilience → mature mainland villa communities, or Saadiyat long hold — FDI and ultra-luxury buyers cluster here; 5+ year hold logic.

Summary

Day 3 does one thing: use ADREC official data to explain who is buying.

Three takeaways:

  1. 2025: 51% Resident Foreign + 38% Emirati + 11% FDI — foreign buyers drive incremental growth, but the base is "people who live here."
  2. China on both FDI and Resident Foreign Top 10 — Chinese buyers aren't bystanders.
  3. 71% off-plan + 87% cash — the core is cash positioning, betting on city delivery, not short-term gambling.
